Unfortunately for Ismo, lexicographer Jonathon Green has beaten him to the punch: Green’s Dictionary of Slang, the most comprehensive collection of English slang in the world, contains over 140,000 words, including “ass.”

Instead of just offering definitions, Green’s relies on historical citations from documents dating back centuries to show how slang evolves. Green is a linguistic detective, combing through text ranging from 16th century religious pamphlets to internet message boards of the late aughts in search of new slang words and usages.

Green’s is fundamentally different than a traditional dictionary, which is prescriptive: it tells you how words should be used. Green’s is descriptive: it shows how words have been used.

Let’s be descriptivists, and look at the ten different ways Ismo uses the word “ass” in his routine.

The first time Ismo uses the word “ass,” he’s using it to mean “butt.” This is, of course, one of the primary usages of the word “ass,” and one of the oldest. The first recorded usage of this sense comes from a collection of British song lyrics published in 1761.

As we see in Green’s, “ass” is an old word. Its longevity is part of the reason it’s so complicated: over the years, it’s accrued many meanings different forms of usage. Ismo uses “ass” in five different ways: as a noun, an adjective, a suffix, to form a compound word, and as part of a phrase.

To a slang expert like Green, it’s no surprise that “ass” has accrued so many meanings. The word sits in the center of the potent slang venn diagram — it’s related to sex, it’s considered a curse word, and it’s metaphorically evocative, so it can easily be combined with other words to form new phrases and compounds. “Ass” is not so much exceptional as it is representative of the slang ethos.



We can look at other popular slang words to see how these features help terms accrue additional senses. Words like “fuck” (146 senses) and “dick” (120 senses) fulfill the same conditions as “ass,” with a breadth of meaning unique to the sexually explicit and obscene.

Words like “dog” (164 senses) and “cat” (104 senses) aren’t curse words, but they draw upon a trove of evocative animal metaphors to form new usages. The same metaphorical heft contributes to the varied usages of visually stimulating words like “black” (131 senses) and “red” (104 senses).

As it turns out, “ass” is not the most complicated word in English, at least according to Green’s. That dubious honor goes to the word “shit,” which boasts 295 senses, complete with all sorts of befuddling contradictions (“piece of shit” vs. “the shit”).

The reason “ass,” and it’s more complicated counterpart, “shit,” are difficult words to pin down is that slang is fundamentally different than standard English. “The acknowledged nature of language is that it tends to trickle down,” Green says. “Slang does not: it pushes upwards. If it is the language of the gutter, then, looking to impose itself on popular speech, it reaches for the stars.” Slang is democratic; there are no gatekeepers and grammar nazis here. People are coming up with new usages for the word “ass” every day; now that’s some inspiring-ass shit.
